The greenhouse effect

CO2 is a greenhouse gas, which means that it absorbs and emits longwave radiation. This stops some of the radiation from escaping to space, keeping the surface and troposphere warm. Atmospheric CO2 concentrations have been increasing strongly since pre-industrial times, when there were about 280 ppmv. This increases the greenhouse effect, causing an increase in the temperature of the surface and troposphere.

Water vapour is also a greenhouse gas, so an increase in water vapour also causes surface and tropospheric warming. Moreover, as the atmosphere warms, the saturation humidity increases. On average, the atmosphere is not saturated, but it is expected that the amount of water vapour in the troposphere will increase such that the relative humidity remains constant.

As the troposphere warms, the change in temperature varies with height, so the upper troposphere warms more than the surface. This variation influences both the greenhouse effect and humidity and this is known as the lapse rate feedback.
